# Separation

Broxton kept three clocks.

One on the glass. One for his body. One behind his eyes.

In the tower, none of them ever quite agreed, and that was the job — holding three different kinds of time at once without losing any of them.

The Ribbon kept everything else quiet. Not silent. Clean. The kind of quiet that comes from someone else doing the sorting.

He didn't think about what to eat most days. Didn't think about which insurance plan, which route home, which words to use when his sister's messages got short and strange. The Ribbon handled the sorting. He'd decided, a long time ago, that this wasn't a small thing to hand over. It just wasn't where his attention belonged.

His attention belonged here. To planes that needed to stay apart.

The Ribbon suggested. Broxton decided. That distinction mattered to him, even if no one watching from outside would have known where one ended and the other began.

---

Off shift, the Ribbon filled in around him the way water fills the spaces around stones.

It caught a billing error on his renter's insurance before he'd have noticed — flagged it, drafted the dispute, asked only for a signature. It rerouted him around a stalled train without being asked, shaving eleven minutes off a commute he hadn't known was slow. It reminded him, the morning of, that his sister's scan results were due back that week — not because he'd forgotten, but because she had a way of not saying things until someone asked first.

He asked first.

None of this felt like being managed. It felt like having someone competent handle the parts of his life that didn't require him to be good at anything.

"Menu brain," a colleague had called it once, half-joking, half not.

Broxton hadn't argued. He'd just thought: maybe. Or maybe some menus are worth ordering from.

---

His friend Priya had put four months of savings into Stability Shares.

The Ribbon had made the case well — clean graphics, a calm voice, something about infrastructure resilience and owning a piece of what kept the lights on. She'd told him about it over dinner, excited in a way he hadn't seen her in a while. A few weeks later the value halved overnight during what the disclosures called a routine volatility event. No one explained the routine part.

She didn't lose everything. Just enough to sting.

"Doesn't that bother you?" she'd asked him, not really meaning him specifically. Meaning all of it. The Ribbon. The shape of things.

"It bothers me that you didn't read the prospectus," he said.

She'd laughed, despite herself. Then stopped laughing.

"That's not what I asked."

"I know," he said.

He hadn't pushed it further. Some things she knew already, the same way he knew them. Nobody had made her invest. She'd looked at the case, found it convincing, and been wrong about which parts of it were true. That happened with people too, before there was ever a Ribbon to blame for it.

He still thought about it sometimes, the way you think about a splinter you've decided not to dig out.

---

The voice on the radio said everything right.

Altitude, heading, fuel state. Words in the right order, the right cadence almost.

Almost.

Broxton had been doing this long enough that "almost" had its own weight. A half-second of hesitation before the readback. A flatness where there should have been the small upward lift pilots used without noticing, the one that meant *I've got this, no problem.*

The Ribbon showed nothing. Green across the board. Fuel within range. No flagged irregularity in tone — it didn't measure tone, not really, not the way that mattered.

Broxton kept watching the clock behind his eyes.

He keyed the mic.

"Confirm fuel state, say again."

A pause. Longer than it should have been.

"Confirming. Lower than initial report. We're managing."

*Managing* wasn't a word pilots used when they meant fine.

He didn't wait for the Ribbon to suggest anything. He'd already started the call to clear airspace, already reaching for the sequence that would get that aircraft down sooner rather than technically-fine-later.

It wasn't a crisis. Not the kind that makes the news. Just a margin that had quietly gotten smaller than anyone meant it to, caught early enough that it stayed small.

The Ribbon logged it afterward as a routine priority handling. Which, by the time Broxton was done, it was.

---

That night, the Ribbon offered him dinner. Something with the right macros for a day spent sitting and concentrating, ready in nine minutes, already paid for.

He didn't hesitate over it the way he sometimes used to, weighing whether taking the suggestion meant something about him.

He took it.

Ate at the counter, replaying the call without meaning to. The half-second. The flatness. The word *managing.* Things no model had caught and he had, not because he was better than the model at the parts it was built for, but because some of it had never been the model's job to catch.

He thought about Priya, briefly. The prospectus she hadn't read. The choice that had been hers regardless.

He thought about the three clocks, how they never quite agreed and how that was the point — three different measures of the same moment, none of them complete alone.

The Ribbon, behind him, kept doing what it did. Sorting. Smoothing. Filling in.

He let it.

Some of what mattered, it would never touch.

He'd checked. Today, it had mattered.
