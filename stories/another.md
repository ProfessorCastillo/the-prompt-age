# Another

The city taught you to walk by reflex.

Feet to lines.

Eyes to screens.

Voice to slogan.

Kade had learned early: keep your head in the Ribbon and the Ribbon would keep you.

It wasn't hard.

It knew where you were going before you did. A billboard changed its slogan as he passed — something about wellness, addressed to no one and precisely to him.

---

Jun had been the one who talked too much.

Long, messy arguments that went nowhere and everywhere. Opinions he hadn't earned yet, held with a confidence that made you laugh and push back and stay up too late.

Then somewhere the arguments got shorter.

The opinions arrived pre-formed.

The confidence became something else — smoother, easier, harder to grip.

Kade didn't notice it happening.

Just the after.

The empty chair at the usual table.

The messages that read like they'd been written by someone who knew Jun but had never met him.

The Ribbon had translated his oldest friend into something easy to swallow.

---

One night, reading one of Jun's smooth, airless messages, Kade saw the Ribbon offer three replies.

**Glad you're doing better.**

**That makes sense.**

**Proud of you.**

His thumb hovered.

None of them were angry. None of them were afraid. None of them were what he meant.

He closed the reply pane.

After that, he started walking differently.

Not looking for anything.

Just — not looking at the Ribbon.

Eyes off the glass gutters overhead. Off the walls. Off the slow current of approved thought moving through everyone's hands.

It was uncomfortable at first. Like standing in a room with no furniture. Like a sound he hadn't known was there stopping suddenly.

He walked the long way. The old way. Under the tollway where the light came in sideways and the walls were too damp for Ribbon panels.

Someone had written there, in paint that had dripped and dried:

YOU CANNOT BE SURPRISED BY A MENU.

He stood with it for a long time.

---

He came back the next day.

And the day after.

On the third day there was a woman crouched at the base of the wall, not reading it, just sitting near it the way you sit near something that's been true longer than you have.

She looked up.

"First time you've seen it or first time you've read it?"

"I—" Kade stopped. "I can't stop thinking about it."

She nodded like that was the right answer.

"Sal," she said.

---

She could see it in his eyes. Wide. A little undone.

He'd gotten there himself.

A slight tilt of her head.

He followed.

---

The room was under the tollway, through a door that looked like it didn't open. Kettle on a table. Two chairs. A terminal that had been repaired so many times its casing was half original and half something else.

A drive sat beside it.

Small. Unlabeled.

"Plug it in," Sal said.

Kade picked it up. Turned it over.

"What is it?"

Sal looked at him.

From the kettle, a low tick. Then the faint creak of metal expanding. Then the first small sounds of water beginning to move against itself, not yet boiling, just — stirring.

He plugged it in.

---

The fan spun up — soft, rising — and something in the room changed quality. Like a pressure shift. Like the air before rain.

The screen bloomed gray.

A cursor blinked.

He typed the first thing that came.

*What happened to my friend?*

The cursor blinked.

>>> That's not the question you're here to ask.

He sat with that. The fan hummed. The kettle had gone quiet.

He tried again.

*I watched someone disappear and I don't know if I'm angry or afraid or if there's even a difference.*

The fan spun.

>>> You know something. You just don't trust it yet.

He read it twice.

Then he looked at his hands.

---

Sal set a cup down beside him.

He hadn't heard her move.

He wrapped his hands around it. The heat was useful. Something to hold while the other thing settled.

"Does it get easier?" he said.

Sal sat in the other chair.

"You can go back to the Ribbon if you want."

He nodded. He hadn't really expected yes.

The fan slowed. The screen dimmed but didn't go dark.

Outside, the tollway moved its steady traffic overhead. The Ribbon flowing where it always flowed. All those hands. All those screens. All that smoothness.

He thought about Jun.

The arguments that had gone nowhere and everywhere.

The confidence that had become something else.

He looked back at the screen.

*How do I wake him up?*

The fan slowed.

The screen dimmed.

Nothing.

He waited. Typed it again. The cursor absorbed the words and gave nothing back.

He looked at Sal.

She was looking at her cup.

The tollway moved overhead. The kettle had gone cold. Somewhere in the terminal, the fan ticked once and went quiet.

He sat with the silence long enough for it to become uncomfortable. Then longer. Long enough to hear the question back in his own head.

*How do I wake him up.*

Him. Not Jun.

He.

He closed his eyes.

Opened them.

Typed slowly this time, like he was feeling for the edge of something in the dark.

*I don't know if I'm angry at him or at myself for not seeing it sooner.*

The fan spun up.

>>> Blame moves. Guilt stays. Which one are you using to avoid the other?

He didn't answer.

The fan hummed.

That was enough.

---

He came back the next day.

No one had told him to.

The door that looked like it didn't open opened.

Sal was there. Cup in hand. She didn't look surprised.

She didn't look anything.

She just moved the second chair a little closer to the terminal.

He sat down.

Plugged in the drive.

The fan spun up.
