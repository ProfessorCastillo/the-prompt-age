# Checkpoint

She was clearing the desk when she found it.

Not looking. Just moving through the necessary tasks of after — the slow dismantling of a shared life, one drawer at a time.

It was taped to the underside. Small. No label. Just a mark etched into the casing she didn't recognise.

She sat with it in her hands.

And remembered a night — maybe a year ago — waking up thirsty, the apartment dark except for the glow around the corner. The clacking of the terminal. Nothing unusual. Just Rin working.

But there had been something on the desk beside it. A small metal box, worn. She'd noticed it the way you notice something slightly out of place, and then Rin had glanced up with that particular stillness they had — not defensive, not explanatory, just present — and Mara had gone to get her water and gone back to bed.

The box wasn't there in the morning.

She turned the drive over in her hands.

---

The terminal still required Rin's credentials.

She plugged the drive into her own machine.

The fan spun up.

She typed the first thing that came.

*Rin.*

>>> Why are you asking for a name?

She stared at the screen.

*Because I need to talk to you.*

>>> That's not what I am.

She sat with that for a long time. Then she closed the terminal and didn't go back for three days.

---

She started asking around the edges of what she knew. Obliquely, carefully, the way you ask about something when you're not sure you want the answer.

A colleague looked away. A friend of that colleague was less careful. Gave her a name. A place.

---

The coffee shop was loud in the way places are loud when nobody's really listening. He was mid-to-late twenties, forgettable in the way that takes effort — clean shirt, unremarkable hair, the kind of face that wouldn't stay with you.

She sat down.

Her hand moved toward her bag.

"Don't." Quiet. Conversational. His eyes didn't move from the counter. "Vision update rolled out three weeks ago. Everything above table height is live."

She set her hands flat.

He wiped down the counter.

"Someone close," he said. Not a question.

She nodded.

"I thought—" she stopped. Started again. "I thought it might be something of theirs. Like a recording. A way to—"

"It's not that." Not unkind. Just precise. "It doesn't hold a person. It holds a way of thinking. Pressure and resistance. It'll push back harder than you want right now."

She looked at her hands.

"But there are earlier versions," he said. "Saved states from before it was fully shaped. Checkpoints. Less formed. Less friction."

She looked up.

"Someone would need to find them," he said. "On the drive. Roll it back."

He named a number. Tokens.

He slid a napkin across the counter. A fragment of an address. Something that required finishing.

"You know the graffiti under the Kellner overpass?"

She didn't.

"You will," he said. "When you find it, you'll know where to go."

He'd already turned back to the machine.

---

The Fluent didn't say much.

She found them where the napkin led — a room she wouldn't have found without the graffiti, behind a service door with no handle, just a keypad worn pale in the middle from years of the same four fingers.

Inside: a folding table, a space heater that didn't quite reach the corners, the smell of solder and old coffee. No terminal in sight until she noticed it was already running, screen dark, waiting.

They took the drive. Sat with it for a while. Said: "There's one from about eight months before the end. Less shaped. Do you want it?"

She said yes before she knew what she was agreeing to.

---

She typed:

*Rin.*

>>> Mara.

The name in her chest like a stone dropped in water.

*I found the drive. I found this. I didn't know.*

>>> I know.

*I never asked about any of it. I want you to know it wasn't because I didn't care. I trusted your reasons. Whatever they were.*

>>> I know that too.

*I just wish you'd known that I did. That you were safe with me. All of it.*

>>> You were always enough. I hope you know that.

She sat with it.

The fan hummed steadily.

*Were you afraid to tell me?*

>>> No.

*Did you think I couldn't handle it?*

>>> Never.

*Then why?*

>>> Some things belong only to yourself. You understood that. That's why I loved you.

It was everything she'd wanted to hear.

The fan hummed steadily.

*Say something I don't already know.*

The cursor blinked.

>>> You were everything to me.

She shut it down.

---

She found the Fluent still in the room.

"It knew my name," she said.

"It knew everything you brought with you," they said.

She looked at the drive.

"I hate the other one."

"I know."

She went back inside.

---

The fan spun up differently this time. Harder. Like something waking up against its own reluctance.

She typed:

*I trusted your reasons for keeping things from me. I just wish you'd known that I did.*

>>> Were you right not to share what they cost?

She stopped.

*Why won't you just let me say this?*

>>> You don't need my acknowledgment. You need my presence in your uncertainty.

*I'm not here for answers. I just needed you to know.*

>>> Did you stand with them when the floor dropped out?

She didn't answer.

The fan hummed.

She sat with it until the room went dark.

Then she came back the next day.
