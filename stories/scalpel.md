# Scalpel

Solange had forty minutes.

The last patient had run long — a woman with chest pain that turned out to be grief, which took longer to rule in than a heart attack would have. You couldn't rush that part. You couldn't hand it off. By the time she'd walked the woman out and closed the clinic, the town hall was forty minutes from starting and she was still in her coat with the smell of the waiting room on her.

She sat down at the terminal in the back office.

The drive was where it always was. She'd never named it anything. When anyone asked, she called it her notes. It was easier than the truth, which was that forty years of her own judgment lived on it — every differential she'd second-guessed, every case that had gone wrong and taught her something, the whole long argument she'd been having with herself since her residency. She didn't ask it what to do. She'd learned a long time ago what happened when a doctor let the machine decide.

She'd watched it happen. A colleague, early, a forward field hospital — a man who ran everything through a model and called it efficiency. Notes, triage, dosing. He stopped holding the reasoning himself because the tool would hold it for him. Three people died before anyone caught what he'd stopped doing. Negligence, the review called it. She'd always thought the word was too small. He hadn't been careless, but he'd never felt the moment he stopped deciding.

She had no notes and a case she'd been building in her head for weeks. She ran it the only way she trusted.

The fan spun up.

*The policy would ban the offline tools I use,* she typed. *They're offering a subsidized replacement that runs on their infrastructure and reports back. Mine would become illegal, and there's no way to move forty years of this into their version — it isn't built to hold what I know, it's built to replace it. I'm going to argue against the ban tonight. Be the person in that room I most need to answer. Not the easy one. The one I'm not sure I can beat.*

She made her case. She was good at it. Disciplined use. A tool for thinking, not a substitute for it. Accountability that never left her hands.

The cursor held. Then it stopped being her tool and became someone else.

>>> I don't like their system reporting back either. That's not the part that scares me.

>>> You're asking us to keep legal a private medical tool nobody can inspect, nobody can test, that only you understand. You say it's your judgment and not a replacement for it. Maybe that's true for you. But how am I supposed to know that? How is any patient supposed to know?

>>> The next doctor will say exactly what you said. Trust me, I built my own, it has my experience in it. How do we tell the careful ones from the rest? You say forty years like it should settle it. The law doesn't get to meet you. It meets everyone who'll say what you just said.

>>> Don't answer me like you're exceptional. Answer me for the worst doctor in town who wants the same permission.

There it was.

Not a surprise. She'd known it would be this one — she'd known the way she knew a certain chest pain was the heart before the reading came back. You didn't run the case to be surprised. You ran it to see the shape of the thing clearly, so you could stand in front of it without flinching.

She looked at the objection for a moment. Then she closed the terminal, because she was out of time, and because she didn't need it anymore. It had done its job. It had shown her exactly where the room would push.

Now she had to decide whether she had anything to push back with.

---

She walked.

Four blocks, cold, her hands in her coat, and she built it the way she'd been building it for weeks.

Trust me isn't good enough. Fine. But it used to be. That was the thing nobody in that room would remember — that trust me had been the entire contract, not just in medicine, in every field that asked a layperson to accept what an expert had spent a life learning. You trusted the doctor, the engineer, the pilot, because trust was the only bridge across a gap of knowledge too wide to cross any other way. It worked for a century. It built the world.

It hadn't collapsed because the technology arrived. It had collapsed earlier, quietly, when people stopped trusting each other and then stopped trusting anyone who claimed to know more than they did. The technology walked into a house already burning.

And her answer was the answer every field had eventually found when a powerful new tool arrived. You didn't ground aviation because a pilot stopped flying the plane and let the autopilot fly it into a mountain. You held the practitioner accountable for the practitioner's choices. Nobody had ever proposed banning the scalpel because a surgeon was negligent with one. You didn't take the instrument away from every steady hand to stop the one that shook. Her colleague hadn't been killed by a model. He'd been killed by a handoff he never felt himself make — the tool didn't take his responsibility, it just made setting it down feel like nothing at all. That was the danger. Not that the tool decided. That you couldn't feel the moment it started to.

She felt it. Every time. That was the case. She stayed awake to the exact moment he'd slept through, and she stayed awake to it on purpose — what am I missing, what am I missing — because the whole discipline was refusing to stop asking. By the time she could see the union hall ahead of her, lit up and already full, she believed it.

Then she reached the door, and the case ran out.

---

Because she could tell them the ban was wrong. She could stand up, forty years behind her, and say you are aiming at the tool when you should be aiming at the person holding it, and she would be right.

And someone on the council would say: then what should we do instead?

And she didn't know.

That was the edge of it. That was the exact border of forty years — she knew medicine, she knew her own accountability down to the bone, she knew the ban was solving the wrong problem. She did not know what the right policy was. Licensing. Audits. Liability with teeth. Some standard that could tell her from the worst doctor in town without banning them both. That wasn't her field. She had spent her life becoming an expert in something next to the question, not the question itself.

She had never in her life stood up to object to something without knowing what should be done instead. It went against everything the work had taught her. You didn't tell a patient what they had without telling them what came next.

But no one else was going to say the true thing. She was fairly sure of that. The room was full of people who trusted the tool too much and people who feared it too much, and almost no one who had spent forty years learning the narrow, unglamorous discipline of using a powerful thing without surrendering to it.

She was going in. She'd known that four blocks ago, maybe longer. That was never the question.

The question was whether she could stand up in front of all of them and say the ban was wrong, and then, when they asked her what was right, let them watch her not know. Stand there in the silence after the objection with nothing to fill it. Be an expert at the exact edge of her expertise, in public, with her name on it.

The meeting was already going. She could hear it through the door.

She stood on the pavement in her coat, the clinic still on her, her hand not yet up, and reached for the door.
