# Session

Jhon had been doing this for three years.

Not the underground work — not yet, or not only. Drives still passed hand to hand in the usual quiet way, but he'd started something else alongside it: open sessions in The Flats, advertised plainly, no graffiti required. A room, some chairs, an hour of talking honestly about the Ribbon — what it did well, what it cost, how to tell the difference. People came because a neighbor mentioned it, or because the flyer used the word "free" instead of anything that sounded like recruitment.

He believed in the slow work. He also believed, some days, that the slow work was too slow.

Before each session he posted a poll in the building's Ribbon thread — the kind that auto-suggested its own options the moment you started typing the question, five tidy choices that felt like they'd written themselves. He'd used them without thinking twice. They were faster than typing it out himself.

This week's came back wrong.

Every option assumed some baseline of use. Convenience. Productivity. Comfort. Nowhere to say *I don't want any of this.* Nowhere for someone who'd already decided, on their own, that the Ribbon wasn't a tool they wanted any relationship with at all.

Someone wrote it underneath the poll, in the thread, in the one space that didn't make them choose.

*no option for ppl who don't want to use it. just another corpo shilling for the ribben*

He read it twice. Then a third time, hoping the third reading would feel different.

It didn't.

---

He didn't go to the others. Not yet.

He went to his own drive — the one he kept for himself, the one nobody else had touched.

The fan spun up.

*I built something to help people think for themselves and someone just told me it does the opposite.*

>>> It's not a failure of design — it's a failure of trust. They don't believe in their own agency.

He felt something in his chest loosen. There it was. Not his fault. Just a roomful of people who hadn't done the work.

He almost closed the terminal there.

*That felt better than it should have.*

>>> What was the first thing you felt? Not the version you've already built to defend yourself.

He sat with that longer than he wanted to.

*Embarrassed. Then defensive. Then like I needed to be right.*

>>> Which one was true before the others arrived?

He didn't have an answer yet.

*Did I take the choice away from them without meaning to?*

>>> You can't decide for them and then absolve yourself of accountability. The choice must be theirs — because you don't know their world.

*Then why does it still feel like an accident?*

>>> Maybe you were afraid of what might happen if they saw clearly enough.

He didn't answer.

The fan hummed.

He didn't reach for the terminal again that night.

---

He thought about it again the next day. And the day after that.

Maybe the person who wrote it didn't need him to be right. Maybe they needed him to be the thing they were fighting — needed someone, anyone, playing the villain, so there was still something to push against. The alternative wasn't agreement. The alternative was nothing left to resist at all, and a quiet drift toward whatever the Ribbon offered next.

If staying the villain in their mind was what kept their guard up, he could live with that.

He just didn't know if that was wisdom or a story he was telling himself so the sting would stop.
